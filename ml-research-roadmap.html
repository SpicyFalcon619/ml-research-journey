A field guide, compiled for one traveler

Charting a course into ML & AI research

You already think in code — JS, PHP — which means the on-ramp here is shorter than it looks. This guide assumes nothing about where the trail ends. It's built to get you fluent enough, fast enough, that the destination becomes obvious on its own.

Follow the path top to bottom. Every numbered basecamp is a stage; every ticket below it is a real, named resource with a working link.

The main trail

Four basecamps, walked in order. Budget roughly 2–3 months at a casual, consistent pace to clear all four.

BASE
00

Python, for someone who already codes

1–2 weeks

Skip beginner-from-zero courses entirely. You need the syntax differences, the scientific stack, and comfort in notebooks — not "what is a variable."

The Python Tutorial docs.python.org

The official tutorial. Fastest way to pick up syntax when you already think procedurally.

NumPy Quickstart numpy.org

Arrays and vectorized operations — the substrate everything else in ML sits on.

pandas: Getting Started pandas.pydata.org

For wrangling real datasets — you'll use this constantly from Basecamp 1 onward.

Math, picked up as you walk

ongoing

Not a gate before basecamp 1 — a supply line that runs alongside the whole trail. Learn each piece when a concept ahead needs it.

Essence of Linear Algebra 3Blue1Brown

Vectors, matrices, dot products — the best visual intuition for this material anywhere, free.

Essence of Calculus 3Blue1Brown

Derivatives and gradients, the part that makes "gradient descent" stop being a magic phrase.

Statistics & Probability Khan Academy

Distributions, expectation, Bayes' theorem — pull up specific lessons as they come up.

BASE
01

Classical machine learning

4–6 weeks

Where you learn what ML actually is, before deep learning. Regression, classification, trees, the full real workflow of clean → train → evaluate → iterate.

Machine Learning Specialization Andrew Ng

Still the gold standard for building real intuition. Free to audit on Coursera.

scikit-learn Tutorials scikit-learn.org

Implement everything from the course above on real data, in the library the field actually uses.

Titanic — Machine Learning from Disaster Kaggle

The classic first project. Boring on purpose — it teaches the workflow, not the glamour.

BASE
02

Deep learning

6–8 weeks

Where it gets fun. Neural networks, and the framework the field has standardized on — PyTorch.

Learn PyTorch for Deep Learning: Zero to Mastery Daniel Bourke

The definitive ground-up guide to building neural networks and standard PyTorch workflows.

Practical Deep Learning for Coders fast.ai

Project-first: you'll train and deploy a real model by lesson two. Fits a coder's instinct to build before fully understanding theory.

Deep Learning Specialization Andrew Ng

More rigorous, theory-first. CNNs, RNNs, and the math underneath each architecture.

Neural Networks: Zero to Hero Andrej Karpathy

Builds backprop and a GPT from scratch, in code, line by line. The single best "I actually understand this now" resource.

BASE
03

ML Engineering & MLOps

2–4 weeks

Taking models out of local notebooks and into production. Tracking experiments, deploying APIs, and managing model lifecycles.

Full Stack Deep Learning FSDL

The premier course on what happens after you train a model. Serving, monitoring, and scaling.

Weights & Biases (W&B) wandb.ai

The industry standard for experiment tracking. Stop using spreadsheets to record your loss curves.

The fork in the trail

Once the fundamentals click, you'll notice yourself pulled one direction. That pull is the answer to "what kind of AI work do I actually want to do".

If you're drawn to images

Computer Vision

Signal: you want to see what the model sees, and care how a pixel becomes a prediction.

CS231n: Computer Vision Stanford

Lectures, notes, and assignments are all free and public.

If you're drawn to language

NLP & LLMs

Signal: you keep wanting to build chatbots, text tools, or fine-tune something on your own data.

CS224n: NLP with Deep Learning Stanford

From word vectors to transformers to modern LLMs.

The LLM Course Hugging Face

Hands-on companion — fine-tune real transformer models.

If you're drawn to making things

Generative & Diffusion Models

Signal: you want to generate images, music, or worlds.

Deep Learning to Stable Diffusion fast.ai Part 2

Builds diffusion models from scratch.

If you're drawn to decision making

Reinforcement Learning

Signal: you want AI agents that learn by interacting with environments (games, robotics).

Spinning Up in Deep RL OpenAI

The canonical introduction to modern deep reinforcement learning concepts and math.

The ultimate destination

Reading Papers & Core Research

Signal: you want to implement things that aren't in libraries yet.

How to Read a Paper S. Keshav

Stop reading linearly. Learn the 3-pass method to digest dense research efficiently.

Hugging Face Daily Papers Hugging Face

The firehose of new research (arXiv cs.LG) curated into a digestible daily feed.

Attention Is All You Need arXiv · Vaswani et al., 2017

The paper that introduced the Transformer. Everything from BERT to GPT to Claude traces back to this one.

Deep Residual Learning for Image Recognition arXiv · He et al., 2015

Introduces ResNet and skip connections — the trick that made truly deep networks trainable.

Trail wisdom

How to actually walk this, not just read the map.

Build after every module, not just at the end. The single biggest predictor of who finishes this trail and who doesn't is whether they apply each concept to a tiny project immediately.

Tie a project to something you actually care about. Build on a game-adjacent or creative dataset. It will teach you more than another anonymous Titanic notebook — because you'll actually want to finish it.

Let the fork resolve itself. You don't need to know your destination before setting out. By the end of Basecamp 2, you'll know whether you're chasing research, building products, or just satisfying curiosity.
